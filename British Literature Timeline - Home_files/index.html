Beowulf

By unknown author

An epic poem chronicling the adventures of a powerful Scandinavian hero named Beowulf, who fights a vicious demon named Grendel. The great mead-hall of Hrothgar, king of the Danes, is under attack, and Grendel is terrorizing the Danes at night. Beowulf comes to the rescue, kicking butt for pride, glory, and gold.

  • Highly stylized writing exhibits kennings, alliteration, Anglo-Saxon culture
  • Written in Old English; one of few manuscripts to survive

1392

The Canterbury Tales

By Geoffrey Chaucer

A collection of 24 stories mostly written in verse, presented by a group of religious pilgrims as they journey from London to the Canterbury Cathedral. The group represents people from all walks of life; each one tells a short tale, ranging from somber to bawdy, to keep everyone entertained during the journey.

  • Written in Middle English rather than Latin, unusual for the era
  • Look for elements of satire, the use of wit to expose and criticize societal issues

Hamlet

By William Shakespeare

A tragedy set in Denmark about the titular Prince Hamlet, who battles his conscience and indecision to avenge his father’s death. Hamlet falls into a deep depression after his recently-widowed mother marries his uncle Claudius, who Hamlet sees as an usurper. What follows becomes a legendary power struggle of youth, age, conspiracy, and murder.

  • Key themes: Madness, Vengeance, Love, Emotion
  • Look for elements of tragic structure - tragic flaw, increasing isolation, catharsis

1603

Macbeth

By William Shakespeare

Written in 1603, this is the story of a man’s quest for power. After hearing a prophecy that foretells his ascent, the titular character is convinced by his sinister wife to seize the throne by betraying his king. Caught in a web of his own lies, Macbeth must defend his illegitimate title with his life.

  • aka "the Scottish play"
  • Key themes: Fate, Ambition, Betrayal, Sleep
  • Once again, look for elements of tragic structure

1667

Paradise Lost

By John Milton

An epic poem written in blank verse telling the tale Satan: his fall from heaven, the subsequent War in Heaven, and his role in the temptation of Adam and Eve. Separated into twelve books, Paradise Lost continues the Homeric tradition of invoking the muse and opening in medias res. Chock-full of cinematic scenes and powerful imagery.

  • First work to fully develop Satan’s character
  • Much of the Biblican imagery in pop culture is inspired by this - ex. His Dark Materials by Philip Pullman

1726

Gulliver's Travels

By Jonathan Swift

Famous prose story detailing the adventures of Lemuel Gulliver, a run-of-the-mill educated dude who gets shipwrecked on a strange island occupied by a civilization of people who are six inches tall. Hilarity ensues. Look past the pure ridiculousness of the plotline to find biting satire.

  • One of the first of the “travellers’ tales” genre
  • Criticizes human nature and the political climate of England (e.g. Tories vs Whigs)

1757 — 1850

The Romantic Poets

William Blake. John Keats. William Wordsworth. Reacting against the science and cold analytics of the Enlightenment, poets turned to mysticism, individualism, and emotion, giving us such masterpieces as The Tyger and Ode on a Grecian Urn. These ideas still ring true today if you’re a believer in spontaneity and emotion as a source of aesthetic experience.

1813

Pride and Prejudice

By Jane Austen

A rebellious girl named Elizabeth crosses paths with a dude named Mr. Darcy. Rich and handsome, but proud. You already know what’s going down. Darcy begins to fall in love with Elizabeth, and as the story unfolds, Lizzie begins to slowly overcome her initial prejudice toward him, eventually reciprocating his love despite their differences in status and personality.
  • Explores the clash between social classes in 19th century England
  • Just a little bit of snark and satire
  • Brilliant wordplay holds up even today

1892

Tess of the d'Urbervilles

By Thomas Hardy

This novel highlights the role of fate in the corruption of the innocent English peasant girl Tess Durbeyfield. Despite her purity, Tess is thrown into the clutches of injustice where she must battle with gender and class expectations. Through horrifyingly exaggerated realities, Hardy seeks to raise a red flag for the consequences of his society’s sexual standards.

  • Originally rejected by publishers for its controversial take on sex
  • The subtitle “A Pure Woman” was later added to emphasize Tess’s standing

1958

Things Fall Apart

By Chinua Achebe

One of the first novels from an African author to make it big, depicting the daily life and initial effects of colonialism on the Igbo people. It follows the tragic struggle of Okonkwo, a masculine to a fault, hardworking man from the fictional village of Umuofia who is faced with hurdle after hurdle on his path to greatness. Ultimately, he is faced with the greatest hurdle of all: the arrival of the white man.

  • Key themes: Imperialism, Fate, Masculinity
  • Fourth studio album of the band The Roots named after this book
